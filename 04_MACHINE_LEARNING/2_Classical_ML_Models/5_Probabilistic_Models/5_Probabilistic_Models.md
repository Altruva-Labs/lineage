# Chapter 4.2.5: Probabilistic Models

Probabilistic models make predictions by explicitly modeling uncertainty using probability distributions.
They treat the data as being generated by a stochastic process and try to learn the laws of that process.

## 1. Core Idea

At the heart of probabilistic models is the idea that the world is noisy.
Even when you have the right inputs, the output may still be uncertain.

Instead of outputting a single "best guess," probabilistic models often output a distribution or a set of probabilities.

This makes them a natural fit when we care about uncertainty, confidence, or multiple possible outcomes.

## 2. Examples of Probabilistic Models

### Naive Bayes

A very simple and fast classifier.
It assumes that features are conditionally independent given the class.
Despite the strong assumption, it often works well in text classification and other high-dimensional problems.

### Bayesian Networks

These are graphical models that represent joint probability distributions over many variables.
They let you encode dependencies and perform inference about unknown quantities.

### Hidden Markov Models (HMMs)

HMMs model sequences where the system has an underlying hidden state that evolves over time.
They are widely used for speech recognition, part-of-speech tagging, and biological sequence analysis.

### Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs)

GMMs model data as a mixture of several Gaussian distributions.
They are often used for clustering, density estimation, and as a component in more complex methods.

## 3. Strengths

Probabilistic models are powerful when you need:

- explicit uncertainty estimates
- interpretable assumptions about data generation
- principled ways to incorporate prior knowledge

They also connect well to statistics and decision theory.

## 4. Limits

The downside is that probabilistic models can be:

- hard to scale to very large datasets
- sensitive to wrong model assumptions (e.g., independence, distribution shape)
- computationally expensive for exact inference in complex models

## 5. Why This Matters in the Lineage

Probabilistic models show another angle of classical machine learning:
not just making a prediction, but understanding and modeling where randomness comes from.

This way of thinking continues to influence modern tools for uncertainty quantification, Bayesian deep learning, and safety-aware AI.

## 6. What Comes Next

We have now seen three broad ways of building models:
- simple formulas (linear models)
- tree-based rules (decision trees)
- probability distributions (probabilistic models)

Another powerful idea is to look at similarity directly.
That is the core of distance-based and kernel methods.
