# Chapter 4.2.5.3: Hidden Markov Models (HMM)

## The Problem: Sequences, Hidden States, and Time

Most machine learning models we've seen so far make decisions on snapshots of data:
- Linear regression: given features, predict output (one instant)
- Classification: given features, predict class (one instant)

But real data is often sequential:
- **Speech**: phonemes follow phonemes (time matters)
- **Text**: words follow words (context matters)
- **Biology**: DNA bases follow patterns (sequence structure)
- **Stock prices**: tomorrow depends on today

And often, the underlying cause is **hidden** - you can't observe it directly:
- In speech, you hear sound but not the phoneme the speaker intended
- In biology, you see mutations but not the evolutionary force driving them
- In your body's health, symptoms are observable but disease states are hidden

**Hidden Markov Models (HMMs)** model sequences where:
1. There's a hidden state that you can't see
2. That state evolves according to transition probabilities
3. Each state generates an observable output (emission)

---

## What Is an HMM?

An HMM is a probabilistic model with two "layers":

**Hidden state layer**: A sequence of unobserved states:
$$z_1, z_2, z_3, ..., z_T$$

Each state depends on the previous state (the **Markov property**).

**Observable layer**: A sequence of observations generated by the hidden states:
$$x_1, x_2, x_3, ..., x_T$$

Each observation depends only on the current hidden state.

---

## The Markov Property

The key simplification: a state only depends on the **previous state**, not the whole history:

$$P(z_t | z_1, z_2, ..., z_{t-1}) = P(z_t | z_{t-1})$$

This says: "To predict tomorrow's weather, I only need today's weather - the history doesn't add new information."

In reality, this is often not perfect, but it's a useful simplification.

---

## Three Components of an HMM

An HMM is fully defined by three probability distributions:

### 1. Initial State Distribution $\pi$

$$\pi(z_1) = P(Z_1 = z_1)$$

The probability distribution over the first hidden state.

Example: In weather, what's the probability the first day is sunny, rainy, or cloudy?

### 2. Transition Probabilities $A$

$$A_{ij} = P(Z_t = j | Z_{t-1} = i)$$

The probability of transitioning from state $i$ to state $j$.

Example: If today is sunny, what's the probability tomorrow is sunny, rainy, or cloudy?

Often represented as a **transition matrix**:

$$A = \begin{pmatrix}
P(\text{sunny}|\text{sunny}) & P(\text{rainy}|\text{sunny}) & P(\text{cloudy}|\text{sunny}) \\
P(\text{sunny}|\text{rainy}) & P(\text{rainy}|\text{rainy}) & P(\text{cloudy}|\text{rainy}) \\
P(\text{sunny}|\text{cloudy}) & P(\text{rainy}|\text{cloudy}) & P(\text{cloudy}|\text{cloudy})
\end{pmatrix}$$

### 3. Emission Probabilities $B$

$$B_{js} = P(X_t = s | Z_t = j)$$

The probability of observing output $s$ when in hidden state $j$.

Example: If the true weather is sunny, what's the probability you observe "person outside without umbrella", "person outside with umbrella", or "person inside"?

## Real-World Example: Coin Flip Sequence

Imagine a **hidden** coin:
- The coin is either fair (50/50 heads/tails) or biased (80/20 heads)
- Unknown to you, the coin-flipper switches between coins sometimes
- You only see the flips (heads or tails), not which coin was used

**Hidden states**: {Fair, Biased}

**Observations**: {Heads, Tails}

**Transition probabilities**: 
- P(Fair → Fair) = 0.8 (stay with fair coin)
- P(Fair → Biased) = 0.2 (switch to biased)
- P(Biased → Fair) = 0.3 (switch to fair)
- P(Biased → Biased) = 0.7 (stay with biased)

**Emission probabilities**:
- P(Heads | Fair) = 0.5
- P(Tails | Fair) = 0.5
- P(Heads | Biased) = 0.8
- P(Tails | Biased) = 0.2

Now you observe: H, H, T, H, H, H, T

**Question**: What sequence of coins most likely generated this?

Maybe: Fair, Fair, Fair, Biased, Biased, Biased, Biased (the biased coin produces more heads).

## Three Key Problems for HMMs

### Problem 1: Likelihood

Given the model parameters and observations, what's the probability of the observations?

$$P(X_1, X_2, ..., X_T | \text{model})$$

Used to compare different HMM models.

**Algorithm**: Forward algorithm (dynamic programming, O(n²T))

### Problem 2: Decoding

Given observations, what's the most likely sequence of hidden states?

Given: H, H, T, H, H, H, T

Unknown: Which coin (Fair/Biased) was used at each step?

**Algorithm**: Viterbi algorithm (finds best path through the hidden states)

### Problem 3: Learning

Given observations but unknown parameters, learn the transition and emission probabilities.

**Algorithm**: Baum-Welch algorithm (an Expectation-Maximization algorithm, like we saw with GMM)

## Real-World Application: Speech Recognition

In speech recognition:

**Hidden states**: Phonemes (the sounds in speech: /a/, /p/, /t/, etc.)

**Observations**: Audio features (spectral content, frequency patterns)

**Transition probabilities**: Some phoneme sequences are more common. /p/ followed by /a/ is common; /p/ followed by /d/ is rare.

**Emission probabilities**: Given a phoneme, what acoustic features do we expect?

An HMM for each word chains phoneme HMMs together, creating a likelihood for each word given the audio.

The word with highest likelihood is the transcription.

---

## Strengths

**Natural for Sequential Data**: Designed specifically for sequences where order matters.

**Handles Hidden Structure**: You don't need to observe the underlying cause - the model infers it from observations.

**Probabilistic Framework**: Based on well-understood probability theory.

**Interpretable**: The transition and emission probabilities have clear meanings.

**Proven Effective**: Dominant approach for speech and bioinformatics for decades.

---

## Limitations

**Markov Assumption**: The next state depends only on the current state, not the history. Real sequences often have longer-range dependencies.

Example: In language, "the cat sat on the..." - the word "cat" influences "sat" even if they're separated by other words.

HMMs would forget "cat" influence once you reach other words.

**Finite Memory**: Related to above - HMMs have no long-term memory.

**Scalability**: Complex sequences need many states, which requires lots of data to estimate.

**Discrete Hidden States**: Classic HMMs assume hidden states are discrete. Continuous states (like real-valued positions) require extensions.

---

## Connection to What Came Before

Bayesian Networks modeled static relationships: given variables, compute probabilities.

HMMs add dynamics: variables evolve over time according to transition probabilities.

This is the first classical model we've seen that fundamentally thinks in sequences and time.

**This idea will be central later**:
- Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) learn hidden states and transitions from data
- Transformers build on sequence modeling
- Large Language Models predict the next token given previous tokens (like an HMM on steroids, with learned transitions)
