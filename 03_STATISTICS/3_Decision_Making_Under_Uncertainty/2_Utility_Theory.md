# Chapter 3.3.2: Utility Theory

Probability tells an agent what may happen.
But that is not enough to choose well.

The agent also needs to know:

> which outcomes it prefers

That is the role of **utility**.

A utility is a number that represents how desirable an outcome is to the agent.

Higher utility means:

- more preferred
- more useful
- more valuable for the goal

## 1. Simple Illustration

Suppose a robot can choose between two delivery routes.

Possible concerns include:

- speed
- safety
- battery use

A route that is fast but risky may not be better than a route that is slightly slower but much safer.

Utility gives the agent a way to compare these tradeoffs with one scale.

## 2. Why Utility Matters

Without utility, probability alone is incomplete.

Example:

- Outcome A may be very likely
- Outcome B may be less likely

But if Outcome B is far more valuable, the best decision may still favor it.

So decision-making needs both:

- belief about what may happen
- value placed on what happens

## 3. Risk and Preference

Utility also helps capture attitudes like:

- risk aversion
- cost sensitivity
- reward preference

That is why two agents can face the same probabilities but still choose differently.

They may value outcomes differently.

## 4. Lineage Meaning

This is a key step in AI because it reconnects probability to action.

In early rational-agent thinking, the question was:

> what should the agent do?

Utility theory now gives a more realistic answer:

> the agent should choose actions based on both 'uncertainty' and 'preference'

The next step is to combine those two pieces formally.

That gives us **expected utility maximization**.
